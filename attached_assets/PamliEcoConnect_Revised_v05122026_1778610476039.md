## Executive Summary

PamliEcoConnect is an electric fast-ferry initiative purpose-built for Pamlico Sound, developing a family of quiet, low-wake, high-efficiency passenger vessels in two classes: a 20-passenger R&D platform and a 50-passenger flagship class. The company is structured in two complementary layers — a for-profit operating entity running scheduled and charter ferry services, and a mission-aligned nonprofit partner focused on decarbonization, research, education, and community access.

What makes PamliEcoConnect fundamentally different from a generic ferry startup is that the vessel platform already exists in prototype form. Founder Jay Phillips has designed, built, and operated foiling catamarans in this region for years. The hull molds for the 20-passenger R&D vessel — derived from the Stiletto X foiling catamaran series, a design Phillips developed himself — are already built, on hand at Phillips Boatworks on Hatteras Island, and are being contributed as a founder investment with a current replacement value of $200,000 to $500,000. This means the $1 million seed round is not funding a design exercise. It is funding construction of the first four vessels using proven molds, by a builder who has done it before.

The 20-passenger vessels are the essential R&D platform for this program. They will prove the route economics, the technology, the regulatory pathway, and the operational model before the company scales to the 50-passenger flagship fleet. Critically, these vessels can begin operating as 6-passenger uninspected vessels under existing USCG rules as soon as they are built, generating ticket revenue before full 20-passenger certification is complete. This dramatically reduces the gap between investment and cash flow.

The financial model has been stress-tested across three scenarios — conservative, base, and optimistic — reflecting realistic ranges for occupancy, trip frequency, and seasonal demand. Even the conservative scenario demonstrates strong unit economics from a small initial fleet.

Beyond ferry operations, PamliEcoConnect has multiple revenue streams that are not typical of a ferry startup: vessel sales to third-party operators ($400,000 to $1,200,000 per 20-passenger vessel, $1,500,000 to $2,000,000 per 50-passenger vessel at approximately $750,000 build cost), recreational variants using the same hull molds with different bridgedeck configurations, and a significant Jones Act competitive advantage that protects the domestic market for U.S.-built vessels.

The Chesapeake Bay connection is more than a market analogy. A five-county tourism consortium in Maryland — Anne Arundel, Calvert, Queen Anne's, Somerset, and St. Mary's counties — has completed a U.S. Department of Commerce-funded feasibility study finding passenger ferry service on the Chesapeake Bay to be viable, has received $3.9 million in federal funding for electric passenger ferries, and is actively moving toward vessel procurement. PamliEcoConnect CFO John Edward Elion, who serves as Director of the Annapolis-based Waterworks Foundation and has worked on Chesapeake Bay environmental and maritime initiatives for over 30 years, was instrumental in stimulating that consortium's discussions and maintains active relationships with its leadership. PamliEcoConnect's 50-passenger foiling vessel is designed to be exactly what that consortium needs. Pamlico Sound is the proving ground. Chesapeake Bay is the first major commercial contract opportunity.

PamliEcoConnect is raising $1,000,000 in seed capital to commence vessel construction, begin the USCG certification process simultaneously for both the 20-passenger and 50-passenger platforms, improve the Buxton landing zone, and fund initial marketing and operations through 2026-2027.

## Company Overview

Mission

To provide fast, clean, and locally appropriate water-based transportation that connects Inner Banks and Outer Banks communities, reduces emissions and wake impacts across Pamlico Sound, and creates tangible economic and educational opportunities in coastal North Carolina.

Business Structure

PamliEcoConnect is structured as a platform, not a single boat, organized in two complementary layers.

The for-profit operating entity is responsible for the day-to-day business of running passenger ferry service: vessel operations and crewing, ticketing and revenue management, scheduling and route optimization, charter operations, and regulatory compliance.

The mission-driven partner layer is a nonprofit or foundation alignment focused on impact and long-term sustainability: research and demonstration projects on electric propulsion and hydrofoil wake and shoreline effects, educational programs and youth engagement, workforce development, and community access and philanthropic support.

This dual structure allows PamliEcoConnect to speak to both commercial investors and mission-aligned funders simultaneously, which is the appropriate architecture for a venture at the intersection of clean transportation infrastructure, regional mobility, and coastal economic development.

John Edward Elion, PamliEcoConnect's CFO, serves as Director of the Waterworks Foundation, an Annapolis-based environmental nonprofit with over 30 years of active work on Chesapeake Bay ecological and maritime issues. The Waterworks Foundation represents a natural model and potential partner for PamliEcoConnect's nonprofit layer, and Elion's leadership of both organizations creates a direct bridge between the Pamlico Sound initiative and the established Chesapeake Bay environmental and maritime community.

Strategic Positioning

PamliEcoConnect sits at the intersection of three converging trends: clean transportation and decarbonization, regional mobility and tourism, and local expertise with national relevance. The company is rooted in Hatteras Island and Pamlico Sound but is part of a broader class of electric fast-ferry initiatives emerging along the U.S. East Coast.

Key Advantages

First-mover potential for electric and foil-assisted passenger service on Pamlico Sound. Proven hull platform already in mold form, contributed by the founder with $200,000 to $500,000 replacement value. Active relationship with the federally funded Chesapeake Bay ferry consortium through CFO John Elion. Jones Act compliance built into the design and manufacturing strategy, creating a domestic market moat. Multiple revenue streams beyond ferry operations, including vessel sales and recreational variants. A family of vessels serving multiple market segments from the same core hull platform.

## Market Analysis

Regional Context — Pamlico Sound

Pamlico Sound is one of the largest estuarine systems in the United States, separating the Outer Banks barrier islands from the Inner Banks mainland. Despite this natural water highway, mobility is dominated by long, indirect highway trips and vehicle ferries optimized for cars and trucks rather than foot passengers. The geography is ideal for fast, clean passenger service that shortens travel times, improves the visitor experience, and reduces congestion and emissions.

Tourism and Local Demand

The Pamlico, OBX, and IBX region attracts hundreds of thousands of visitors each year, with strong seasonality from spring through fall. Key market segments include family and vacation tourists visiting Outer Banks, Hatteras Island, and Ocracoke; eco-tourism and adventure travelers focused on kayaking, sailing, fishing, and wildlife; residents and workers moving between mainland towns and island communities; and educational and research travelers from universities, field programs, and nonprofits.

Validated Demand: The Chesapeake Bay Parallel

The strongest available evidence for passenger ferry demand in comparable coastal estuarine markets comes from the Chesapeake Bay. In 2024, a five-county tourism consortium — Anne Arundel, Calvert, Queen Anne's, Somerset, and St. Mary's counties, spearheaded by Visit Annapolis and Anne Arundel County — released the results of a U.S. Department of Commerce-funded feasibility study conducted by Cambridge Systematics with naval architecture support from Art Anderson. The study found passenger ferry service feasible and projected approximately 50,000 annual passengers and roughly $11 million in annual economic benefit from visitor spending alone. The consortium subsequently received $3.9 million in federal funding for electric passenger ferries and is actively moving toward implementation.

This is not a hypothetical analogy. It is a federally validated, fully funded program on a directly comparable coastal estuary, targeting the same vessel class PamliEcoConnect is developing. PamliEcoConnect CFO John Edward Elion, through his leadership of the Waterworks Foundation and 30 years of Chesapeake Bay maritime work, was instrumental in stimulating the consortium's discussions and maintains active relationships with its leadership. The same geography, the same market dynamics — seasonal tourism, congested road corridors, underused water routes — exist in Pamlico Sound, with even fewer existing passenger options.

Early Demand Indicators

While formal ridership studies for the Pamlico Sound routes are part of the Phase 1 work plan, the following factors support initial demand assumptions. The NCDOT vehicle ferry system serving Ocracoke and Hatteras consistently operates at or near capacity during peak season, indicating strong latent demand for Sound crossings that the vehicle ferry system cannot fully absorb. The OBX tourism economy draws several million visitors per year, concentrated in a geography where water shortcuts offer meaningful time savings over road routes. The Outer Banks was explicitly cited in a Baltimore Sun reader commentary as the model Chesapeake ferry advocates should follow — a signal that the market already recognizes Pamlico Sound's relevance to this broader trend.

Transportation Gap

There is currently no electric fast-ferry or hydrofoil-based passenger service linking IBX and OBX communities. PamliEcoConnect is designed to occupy this uncontested clean-fast-passenger niche.

Competitive Landscape

NCDOT Ferries are non-competing and a potential partner. They are slow, vehicle-oriented, with fare structures optimized for cars and trucks rather than high-value passenger trips. Local tour operators offer short scenic trips and charters but not consistent intercity transit. Charter and private operators serve private groups and are not scalable public mobility. PamliEcoConnect complements rather than displaces existing services, offering faster and cleaner passenger-focused links, new eco-tourism and experiential travel products, and a platform for public-private collaboration and research.

## Service Model

Proposed Core Routes

Exact routes will be finalized through stakeholder and technical work, but initial focus is on the Vandemere to Ocracoke to Buxton corridor, where water distance offers a meaningful time advantage over road routes and terminals can support safe passenger handling. Supporting routes include shorter 12 to 24 passenger feeder services connecting nearby towns, marinas, or attractions to the main hub, eco-tour loops emphasizing wildlife and maritime culture, and event services tied to regattas, festivals, and waterfront events.

Phased Revenue Launch: The 6-Passenger Bridge

A critical element of the financial model that distinguishes PamliEcoConnect from a conventional ferry startup is the ability to generate ticket revenue before full USCG 20-passenger certification is complete. The Stiletto X-derived vessels qualify as uninspected passenger vessels under the USCG six-passenger rule as soon as they are built. This means the first boats can begin carrying paying passengers — up to 6 per trip — immediately upon launch, building route familiarity, customer relationships, and cash flow while the formal 20-passenger certification process proceeds in parallel. This phased approach substantially compresses the timeline from capital deployment to revenue generation.

Seasonal and Operating Profile

Operating season is initially 8 to 10 months from spring through fall, with potential for year-round service as demand and infrastructure mature. Frequency is 2 to 4 round trips per day on core routes, with additional frequencies on peak days or for events. The winter relocation strategy — moving vessels to a southern market during the NC off-season — improves asset utilization and annual revenue materially, as reflected in the financial scenarios.

Special services include festival and regatta specials, educational and research charters, and private group charters within the same platform.

Ticketing and Pricing

Ticket pricing targets a band equivalent to regional high-value excursions, conceptually in the range of $25 to $45 per passenger for comparable distances, with dynamic pricing for peak days and bundling with lodging, marina, eco-tour, and activity partners.

Customer Experience

The service emphasizes quiet operation relative to diesel craft, with the trip itself positioned as part of the experience. Comfortable cabins with good sightlines, provision for bikes and small gear on some routes, and ADA-aligned design where feasible are design priorities.

## NEW SECTION: Founder Technology Contribution & Vessel Platform
Sidebar label: Vessel Platform

This section addresses a question that sophisticated investors will ask immediately: why is the $1 million seed round enough to build four boats and begin operations, when comparable maritime ventures require multiples of that amount just to reach the water?

The answer is that PamliEcoConnect is not starting from scratch.

The Hull Molds

Jay Phillips, PamliEcoConnect's founder and CEO, has designed and built foiling catamarans for decades. The hull molds for the 20-passenger R&D vessel are derived from the Stiletto X series, a foiling catamaran design that Phillips developed himself. These molds are currently on hand at Phillips Boatworks on Hatteras Island and are being contributed by Phillips as part of his founder investment in the company.

The replacement cost of purpose-built foiling catamaran molds of this quality, in the current market for composite marine tooling, is $200,000 to $500,000. This means the seed round's vessel construction budget is effectively supplemented by $200,000 to $500,000 in contributed founder equity before a dollar of investor capital is spent.

The Stiletto X Platform

The Stiletto X is not a generic catamaran hull. It was designed from the ground up for foiling performance, which makes it directly applicable to PamliEcoConnect's foil-assist ferry concept. Several design features make it particularly well suited for the R&D program.

The vessel is modular and dissassembling by design. The two hulls are connected by carbon fiber beams that can be specified at different lengths, allowing the catamaran beam to be configured at 18 feet, 20 feet, or wider simply by changing beam specifications. This makes it an inherently adjustable R&D platform — not an adaptation of a fixed design but a system built for modification and iteration.

The bridgedeck, hull tops, and foil system are accessory components to the hulls. Different bridgedeck and cockpit configurations allow the same hull molds to produce vessels optimized for passenger ferry service, private charter, or recreational use. This means the mold investment is not single-purpose — it supports multiple product lines and revenue streams simultaneously.

Hull geometry can be modified for the 20-passenger passenger weight requirements through buoyancy extensions — a well-understood naval architecture technique that lengthens or widens the hull form based on USCG stability requirements. This is a bolt-on modification, not a ground-up redesign.

Prior Construction Experience

Phillips has built and equipped vessels using this hull form before, and has trained teams to do so. The construction process is not experimental. The $125,000 per vessel build cost is credible precisely because the molds exist, the process is documented, and the builder has done it.

Multi-Market Revenue from a Single Hull Platform

The Stiletto X hull platform supports multiple revenue streams beyond ferry operations.

Recreational variants using the same molds with different bridgedeck and cockpit configurations carry retail values of $400,000 to $1,200,000 depending on specification and certification level. Commercial ferry variants certified for 20 passengers under USCG COI are in the same value range. The 50-passenger flagship vessel, while a larger platform, uses related design principles and is anticipated to cost approximately $750,000 to build and sell for $1,500,000 to $2,000,000, representing approximately a 100 percent gross margin on the manufacturing side of the business.

Once proven in commercial passenger service, a U.S.-built, Jones Act-compliant foiling catamaran with demonstrated route economics has essentially no domestic competition. Demand from other operators seeking to launch comparable services — including the Chesapeake Bay consortium — represents a significant vessel sales opportunity that sits alongside and is supported by PamliEcoConnect's own ferry operations.

## NEW SECTION: Jones Act Compliance & Domestic Market Position
Sidebar label: Jones Act Strategy

The Jones Act — formally the Merchant Marine Act of 1920 — requires that vessels transporting passengers or cargo between U.S. ports be built in the United States, owned by U.S. citizens, and crewed by U.S. citizens or permanent residents. For most maritime technology ventures, Jones Act compliance is a cost and a constraint. For PamliEcoConnect, it is a competitive moat.

PamliEcoConnect's vessels are designed to be built in the United States, at Phillips Boatworks on Hatteras Island, by American workers using American materials. This is not a compliance accommodation — it is the natural outcome of a founder-led build program rooted in a domestic boatbuilding operation with decades of history on the Outer Banks.

The implications for the business are significant.

Any operator seeking to deploy passenger ferry service on U.S. coastal waters — Pamlico Sound, Chesapeake Bay, Great Lakes, Gulf Coast, Hawaii — must use Jones Act-compliant vessels. This means PamliEcoConnect's vessel platform, once certified and proven in commercial operation, is the natural supply source for any comparable domestic fast-ferry initiative. Foreign-built foiling catamarans, however capable, cannot legally serve these routes.

The Chesapeake Bay consortium, with its $3.9 million in federal funding and five-county backing, will need Jones Act-compliant vessels. The electric ferry programs being developed at ports and waterways across the U.S. East Coast will need Jones Act-compliant vessels. PamliEcoConnect is positioned to be the domestic manufacturer of record for this emerging vessel class.

Crewing requirements under the Jones Act are also addressed by PamliEcoConnect's operating model. The company is building from a base of experienced local maritime workers on Hatteras Island and the broader OBX community, where commercial boating, USCG licensing, and on-the-water experience are common.

## NEW SECTION: USCG Regulatory Pathway
Sidebar label: Regulatory Pathway

Getting a novel electric and foil-assisted passenger vessel certified for commercial operations is one of the highest-probability schedule risks in the PamliEcoConnect program. The company is approaching this risk directly rather than minimizing it.

The 20-passenger vessels will be submitted for USCG Certificate of Inspection under the appropriate subchapter for small passenger vessels. This is not a routine process for a vessel type that combines electric propulsion with hydrofoil or foil-assist systems, and the company is not projecting a routine timeline. Realistic planning assumes 12 to 24 months from initial USCG engagement to COI issuance for the 20-passenger platform, depending on the degree of novel technology review required.

The mitigation strategy has three elements.

First, USCG engagement begins immediately at seed close. A portion of the seed round is specifically allocated to engaging the USCG Marine Safety Office at the outset of the design and construction process, not after the vessel is built. This is the approach that produces the fastest approval timeline — regulators who are involved in the design process are better positioned to issue certificates efficiently than regulators who see a finished vessel for the first time at inspection.

Second, the 20-passenger and 50-passenger certification processes are initiated simultaneously. The regulatory learning and relationship-building from the 20-passenger approval directly accelerates the 50-passenger pathway. This is a deliberate strategy to compress the overall program timeline.

Third, the 6-passenger uninspected vessel pathway provides a revenue bridge. As described in the Service Model section, the vessels can begin carrying up to 6 passengers under existing USCG rules for uninspected passenger vessels as soon as construction is complete, without waiting for COI issuance. This generates ticket revenue, builds route familiarity, and creates operational data that supports the full COI application.

The company has identified engaging a maritime regulatory specialist as a near-term advisory hire. The Compliance Lead role described in the Management Team section is specifically focused on guiding the USCG approval process and insurer expectations from the 20-passenger R&D platform through 50-passenger commercial certification.

Contingency Planning

If USCG certification of the 20-passenger platform takes longer than 18 months, the financial impact is partially offset by 6-passenger operations revenue. If technical issues arise during the certification process that require design modifications, the modular nature of the Stiletto X platform — with its adjustable beam, removable bridgedeck, and bolt-on foil system — means that modifications can be made without scrapping the hull tooling investment. This is a material advantage over a fixed-design vessel program.

## Vessel & Technology Platform

Vessel Family

PamliEcoConnect anticipates a family of vessels optimized for Pamlico Sound conditions and passenger demand, all derived from the Stiletto X hull platform developed by founder Jay Phillips.

The 20-Passenger R&D Class is the foundation of the program. Four vessels will be built in Phase 1 using existing molds at Phillips Boatworks. Build cost is approximately $125,000 per vessel using contributed molds and in-house construction capability. Retail value at 20-passenger COI certification is $400,000 to $1,200,000 per vessel depending on specification. These vessels serve primary routes, feeder services, charters, and R&D functions simultaneously.

The 50-Passenger Flagship Class is the target for the Series A phase. Anticipated build cost of approximately $750,000 per vessel, with a sale value of $1,500,000 to $2,000,000 per vessel. The Chesapeake Bay consortium's vessel requirement is directly aligned with this class.

Technology and Design Principles

Electric or hybrid-electric drive with shore-based charging at primary terminals. Foil assistance to reduce drag and energy use, lower wake and shoreline impact, and improve passenger comfort in chop. Shallow-draft and low-wake geometry tuned to Pamlico Sound constraints. Modular beam and bridgedeck system allowing configuration adjustment without retooling. Safety and redundancy including operation in displacement mode at reduced speed if needed, redundant propulsion and control systems, and conservative operating envelopes for weather and sea state.

Manufacturer and Partner Strategy

PamliEcoConnect will build the initial four 20-passenger vessels in-house at Phillips Boatworks, leveraging existing molds and established construction experience. For the 50-passenger flagship class and potential production scale-up, the company will seek strategic partners for detailed engineering, construction capacity, and lifecycle support. A common platform approach that serves both Pamlico Sound and Chesapeake Bay corridors is a design priority.

## Operations Plan

Staffing

Per vessel: 1 Captain plus 2 Deckhands per sailing day, with relief crew as needed. Dock agents or shared staff at key terminals. Central operations manager and modest administrative support. As the fleet scales, staffing scales in proportion to route count and frequency.

Preventive maintenance built into the schedule. For electric and foil systems, adherence to manufacturer service intervals. Winter layup for deeper maintenance and upgrades. The modular design of the Stiletto X platform facilitates maintenance access and component replacement.

USCG compliance for passenger vessels. Standardized safety training and onboard drills for crew. Weather monitoring protocols and conservative go/no-go policies. Overview

PamliEcoConnect is raising capital in three distinct stages, each matched to a specific phase of company development, a specific investor profile, and a specific risk and return profile. This section describes the rationale for each stage, the valuation methodology, the investor incentives, and the ownership implications — with the goal of giving prospective investors at any stage a clear and honest picture of how their capital fits into the overall program.

The company is structured to allow each stage of investor to participate at a valuation appropriate to the risk they are accepting, with returns that scale accordingly. Series B investors accept a validated, lower-risk opportunity and receive institutional-grade returns with capital preservation features. Founders retain operating control throughout via a dual-class share structure described below.

A Note on Valuation Methodology

Valuing an early-stage maritime infrastructure and manufacturing venture is not a straightforward exercise, and any pre-revenue valuation involves judgment. PamliEcoConnect's seed valuation is supported by four tangible, non-speculative inputs.

First, the contributed hull molds have a documented replacement value of $200,000 to $500,000. This is a hard asset contributed by the founder, not a projection. It forms a real asset floor beneath the valuation.

Second, the Chesapeake Bay consortium's $3.9 million federal grant commitment for electric passenger ferries, combined with a completed feasibility study projecting $11 million in annual economic impact, provides an external, government-validated market reference. PamliEcoConnect's vessel is designed to serve that market. The existence of a funded, adjacent buyer is a material valuation input.

Third, the Year 1 base case operating model projects $3.9 million to $4.2 million in ticket revenue and $1.5 million to $2.0 million in operating profit from four vessels on a single route. Even discounting these projections heavily for execution risk, the present value of a probability-weighted cash flow stream supports a pre-money valuation meaningfully above zero.

Fourth, comparable early-stage maritime and clean transportation ventures have raised seed capital at pre-money valuations ranging from $3 million to $8 million depending on the maturity of the technology and the strength of the founding team. PamliEcoConnect sits in this range with a founder who has already built the hull form, contributed the molds, and established the relevant regulatory and commercial relationships.

Founder Control Throughout

PamliEcoConnect will implement a dual-class share structure at incorporation. Founders receive Class B shares carrying 10 votes per share. Under this structure, founders retain a voting majority through Series B even as their economic ownership is diluted. Investors are protected through the standard mechanisms of preferred stock — liquidation preferences, anti-dilution provisions, and limited protective vetoes on specific material decisions — without requiring voting control to protect their interests.

Protective provisions will be negotiated to be narrow and specific: veto rights over sale of the company, issuance of shares senior to the relevant preferred class, and debt incurrence above a defined threshold. Broad governance vetoes that would allow investors to block ordinary business decisions are not appropriate for this stage and will not be offered.

Opportunity Zone and Maritime Prosperity Zone Tax Incentives for Investors

A significant investor incentive not reflected in the company's own financial statements is the federal tax benefit available to investors who invest through a Qualified Opportunity Fund structure. This benefit is relevant to every seed and Bridge investor who has unrealized capital gains from other investments.

Under the Opportunity Zones 2.0 program enacted in the One Big Beautiful Bill Act on July 4, 2025, the OZ program is now permanent. New zone designations take effect January 1, 2027, with North Carolina nominating approximately 202 census tracts during the July to October 2026 nomination window. Pamlico County and the broader Outer Banks coastal corridor are strong candidates for OZ 2.0 designation given their rural status and economic profile. OZ 2.0 rural investments receive a 30 percent basis step-up after five years and a full fair market value basis step-up after 10 years, effectively eliminating capital gains tax on appreciation for long-term holders.

Separately, the April 2025 Executive Order on Restoring America's Maritime Dominance directed the creation of up to 100 Maritime Prosperity Zones modeled on the Opportunity Zone structure, with the Maritime Action Plan released February 13, 2026 formalizing this directive. MPZs are designed specifically to incentivize domestic shipbuilding, maritime manufacturing, and waterfront infrastructure investment — exactly the activities PamliEcoConnect represents. MPZ boundaries are being finalized by the Secretary of Commerce in 2026. If Hatteras Island or the Pamlico Sound corridor is designated as both an OZ 2.0 and an MPZ, investors could benefit from incentive stacking — combining OZ capital gains deferral and exclusion with MPZ-specific federal incentives.

The practical implication for seed and Bridge investors is that an investment structured through a Qualified Opportunity Fund in a designated OZ or MPZ tract could result in zero federal capital gains tax on the appreciation of their PamliEcoConnect investment held for ten or more years. For an investor with $1 million in unrealized capital gains who reinvests into PamliEcoConnect through a QOF and holds for ten years to a $5 million exit value, the after-tax return under OZ 2.0 would be approximately $4 million in tax-free appreciation rather than $3.2 million after a 20 percent capital gains rate. The company strongly recommends that prospective investors consult with a qualified tax advisor regarding OZ and MPZ eligibility before investing.

PamliEcoConnect is structured as a multi-entity platform designed to serve four distinct constituencies simultaneously: equity investors seeking commercial returns, federal and state grant agencies requiring nonprofit or FAR-compliant structures, public sector partners requiring long-term community commitment, and future geographic expansion requiring isolated operating entities. No single corporate form serves all four. The architecture described here is designed to serve each constituency appropriately while maintaining clean investor economics at the holding company level.

This structure also directly addresses a key investor concern in early-stage ventures: liability concentration. By segmenting manufacturing, operations, and mission activities into separate legal entities, an adverse event in one subsidiary does not automatically threaten the assets or operations of the others.

Tier 1 — PamliEcoConnect Holdings, Inc.

Delaware C-Corporation. This is the entity in which seed, Bridge, and Series B investors hold equity. It is the parent company that owns all operating subsidiaries. Founders hold their equity here. This is the entity that would be acquired, merged, or taken public in any exit scenario.

The Delaware C-Corporation structure is standard for venture-backed companies because it provides the cleanest framework for preferred stock, dual-class shares, option pools, and institutional investment. All investor rights agreements, shareholder protections, and board governance provisions attach to this entity.

Holdings does not employ most operating staff and does not directly hold operating assets. It is a clean holding vehicle whose value derives entirely from the value of its subsidiaries.

Tier 2A — PamliEcoConnect Marine Manufacturing LLC

Wholly owned by Holdings. This subsidiary owns the intellectual property, hull molds, tooling, and manufacturing capability that is the technological foundation of the entire platform.

Key assets held by Manufacturing:
The Stiletto X hull molds, contributed by founder Jay Phillips with a replacement value of $200,000 to $500,000. Any patents or patent applications filed on the foil system, modular beam architecture, or vessel design. Design drawings and engineering documentation. Manufacturing contracts and supplier relationships. Jones Act vessel certification — vessels must be built by a U.S. entity to qualify for Jones Act protection, and Manufacturing is that entity.

Manufacturing contracts with Phillips Boatworks LLC for construction labor and facility use. Phillips Boatworks LLC is Jay Phillips's existing boatbuilding operation on Hatteras Island, which will be converted from its current structure to a formal LLC. This arrangement gives Manufacturing access to an experienced, documented construction team with 20-plus years of history without requiring Holdings to own or capitalize a physical boatyard.

Revenue sources for Manufacturing: vessel sales to PamliEcoConnect operations subsidiaries at internal transfer prices, vessel sales to third-party operators including the Chesapeake Bay consortium, design licensing fees to future operators who build vessels to the PamliEcoConnect specification, and potential defense or government vessel contracts under Jones Act preference.

This structure means that the vessel IP and manufacturing capability can survive independently of the ferry operations. If an operations subsidiary underperforms, Manufacturing retains its assets and can continue selling vessels to other buyers.

Tier 2B — PamliEcoConnect Pamlico Sound Operations LLC

Wholly owned by Holdings. This subsidiary operates the ferry service on Pamlico Sound. It holds USCG Certificates of Inspection for the vessels operating on its routes, employs captains and crew, holds terminal access agreements with NCDOT and local governments, manages ticketing and customer relationships, and reports route-level P&L.

This is the entity that investors in the Pamlico Sound corridor deal with operationally. Future geographic expansions — Chesapeake Bay, Great Lakes, Gulf Coast — would each be a separate Operations LLC, protecting Pamlico Sound investors from exposure to other corridors and vice versa.

Pamlico Sound Operations purchases or leases vessels from Manufacturing at agreed transfer prices, creating an internal market that allows each subsidiary to be valued independently.

Tier 2C — PamliEcoConnect Technology LLC (Optional, Year 2+)

A clean intellectual property holding entity for any patents, proprietary software, or control system technology that emerges from the R&D program. Licensing revenue from Technology to Manufacturing and Operations creates a documented IP value separate from operations, which is useful for future valuation events and for protecting IP in the event of any subsidiary-level distress. This entity is optional at seed stage and should be established when the first patent application is filed.

Tier 3 — The Waterworks Foundation Partnership

The Waterworks Foundation is an Annapolis-based 501(c)(3) environmental nonprofit with over 30 years of Chesapeake Bay environmental and maritime work, directed by PamliEcoConnect CFO John Edward Elion. Rather than creating a new nonprofit from scratch — a process that takes 6 to 12 months and significant legal expense — PamliEcoConnect will establish a formal Mission Services Partnership with the Waterworks Foundation.

Under this partnership structure, the Waterworks Foundation:

Receives and administers grants that require nonprofit status, including NSF education and workforce development grants (ATE program), EPA community environmental grants, and philanthropic donations from individuals and foundations aligned with the clean maritime mission. Many of PamliEcoConnect's most significant potential funding sources — including NSF STTR university partnership grants — require a nonprofit entity as a co-applicant. The Waterworks Foundation fills this role immediately, without the 6 to 12 month IRS determination process a new foundation would require.

Contracts with PamliEcoConnect Pamlico Sound Operations for educational vessel use, research program support, and community access programming. The fee paid by the Foundation to Operations for these services is legitimate commercial income to the operating subsidiary, creating a revenue stream that does not depend on ferry ticket sales.

Provides mission credibility for P3 negotiations with NCDOT Ferry Division, Visit NC, and federal agencies. Government partners are more confident in the long-term community commitment of a venture that has an established environmental nonprofit as a formal program partner. This can accelerate permitting, terminal access negotiations, and public cost-sharing agreements.

Holds community relationships that do not belong on a for-profit balance sheet — dock access agreements with fishing communities, educational partnerships with coastal universities, relationships with Outer Banks and Pamlico Sound conservation groups — that have real operational value to the for-profit entities.

The Mission Services Partnership is documented in a formal agreement specifying the services exchanged, the fees paid, the grant programs administered, and the governance relationship between the Foundation and the Holdings board. John Elion's dual role as CFO of Holdings and Director of the Foundation requires careful conflict of interest management, documented in the Foundation's governance policies and disclosed to all investors.

How the Nonprofit Partnership De-Risks the Investment

The Waterworks Foundation partnership de-risks equity investor capital in three specific ways.

First, it expands the grant capture surface without consuming investor equity. Grants that flow to the Foundation and are then contracted back to Operations supplement the revenue model without diluting investor ownership. The five-year model includes only probability-weighted estimates of grants the for-profit entities could receive directly. Foundation-channeled grants represent additional upside not currently in the model.

Second, it provides institutional credibility that accelerates the public partnership timeline. NCDOT terminal access, Visit NC co-marketing agreements, and federal infrastructure partnerships all move faster when a credible nonprofit with a 30-year track record is a named program partner. Faster P3 agreements mean faster revenue growth for the operating subsidiary.

Third, it creates a mission anchor that strengthens the grant applications for the for-profit entities. Federal grant reviewers consistently score applications higher when there is a clear community benefit layer alongside the commercial activity. The Foundation's involvement in PamliEcoConnect's grant applications — as a co-applicant, letter of support provider, or program partner — materially improves success probabilities for the programs in the grant pipeline.

FAR Accounting Standards and Federal Grant Compliance

Federal grants above $750,000 in cumulative annual awards trigger Single Audit Act requirements under 2 CFR Part 200 (the Uniform Guidance). Compliance with federal cost accounting standards — drawn from FAR Part 31 and the Cost Accounting Standards — is required for cost-reimbursement grants, SBIR Phase II awards, and any future Department of Defense or Navy contracts.

FAR-compliant accounting is not optional for a company pursuing the grant pipeline described in this plan. It is a prerequisite for receiving, retaining, and reporting on the most significant grants available. The company is treating FAR compliance as a core operational capability, not an afterthought.

What FAR compliance requires in practice:

A job cost accounting system that tracks direct costs — labor, materials, subcontractor costs — by specific project or contract, distinct from overhead and general and administrative expenses. Standard small business accounting systems including QuickBooks can be configured to meet these requirements with the right chart of accounts and cost allocation methodology.

A timekeeping system in which all employees record time by project daily, with employee and supervisor certification. This applies to the CEO, operations manager, and all salaried employees when any portion of their time is charged to a federal grant.

Written accounting policies and procedures that document cost allocation methods, define allowable and unallowable costs per FAR Part 31, and establish internal controls over financial reporting.

Consistent cost allocation methodologies for indirect costs — overhead and G&A — applied across all contracts and grants. Rates must be established, documented, and applied consistently.

A pre-award accounting system survey by the Defense Contract Audit Agency is required before the company can receive SBIR Phase II awards or certain cost-reimbursement contracts. Passing this survey requires that all of the above systems are in place and documented before the survey date.

Professional Accounting Resources

PamliEcoConnect is budgeting for professional accounting support from Year 1 at a level appropriate to the company's stage and grant activity. These costs are reflected in the financial model.

Year 1 to 2: $35,000 to $45,000 annually for a CPA firm or government contracting accounting specialist to establish the FAR-compliant accounting system, configure the chart of accounts, draft written accounting policies, set up the timekeeping system, and prepare the company's first financial statements.

Year 3 and beyond: $55,000 to $80,000 annually as grant volume increases, Single Audit requirements potentially trigger, and the complexity of multi-subsidiary consolidated reporting grows.

Ongoing internal accounting support will be handled with a combination of a part-time or fractional controller familiar with government contracting and AI-assisted financial reporting for variance analysis, grant budget tracking, and management reporting. AI tools can materially reduce the labor cost of routine financial reporting and grant budget management, but cannot substitute for a licensed CPA on audit, tax, and compliance matters.

The cost of FAR-compliant accounting is modest relative to the grants it enables. The FTA Electric Ferry Pilot alone, at a 30 percent probability-weighted expected value of $600,000 in Year 3, returns more than ten years of professional accounting costs in a single award.

## NEW SECTION: Corporate Governance Philosophy
Sidebar label: Governance Philosophy

Introduction

PamliEcoConnect is built to last. The corporate architecture described in the preceding section — a Delaware holding company, segmented operating subsidiaries, a nonprofit mission partner, and a construction contractor with deep local roots — is not administrative complexity for its own sake. It reflects a deliberate philosophy about how a company operating at the intersection of public infrastructure, private capital, environmental mission, and community development should be governed.

This section explains that philosophy in plain language. It is addressed to all prospective investors, partners, and public stakeholders.

Mission Durability Before Financial Returns

The communities served by PamliEcoConnect — Hatteras Island, Ocracoke, Vandemere, the Inner Banks waterfront towns — did not generate this idea. Jay Phillips did, after decades of living and working on the water in this region. The mission of clean, fast, locally appropriate water transportation for these communities is not a marketing position. It is the founding reason the company exists.

The corporate structure is designed so that this mission survives the company's growth. The Waterworks Foundation mission services partnership creates community obligations that are contractual, not aspirational. The nonprofit layer holds educational programs, research relationships, and community access agreements that exist independent of the for-profit equity structure. If PamliEcoConnect is acquired, merged, or substantially restructured at any point in its future, those obligations transfer with the business. They are not optional. They are not subject to a future board vote.

This is a deliberate design choice. Investors who are comfortable with that design — who understand that they are investing in a company that takes its community obligations as seriously as its financial obligations — are the investors PamliEcoConnect wants.

Operational Clarity Through Segmentation

The subsidiary structure exists because different parts of the business have genuinely different risk profiles, different stakeholders, and different performance metrics.

Marine Manufacturing is an IP and production business. It is measured by build quality, cost per vessel, time to COI, and gross margin on vessel sales. Its key risks are construction delays, materials cost inflation, and IP challenges. Its key assets are the hull molds, the design IP, and the Jones Act certification.

Pamlico Sound Operations is a service business. It is measured by occupancy rates, on-time performance, customer satisfaction, and route profitability. Its key risks are weather, demand seasonality, and terminal access. Its key assets are the USCG certificates of inspection, the crew relationships, and the route operating history.

These are different businesses. Mixing them in a single entity obscures performance, complicates cost accounting, and makes it harder for both management and investors to understand what is working and what is not. The segmented structure is not defensive complexity — it is management clarity. Each subsidiary has a simple business plan, a simple P&L, and a simple set of success metrics. The Holdings consolidated financials tell the full investor story.

Liability Isolation as Investor Protection

A maritime operations company carries real liability. A vessel incident, a crew injury, a dock failure — these are not theoretical risks. They are the ordinary hazards of commercial passenger vessel operations. If PamliEcoConnect operated as a single entity, a serious liability event in operations could threaten the manufacturing assets, the IP, the vessel designs, and the capital raised for future expansion.

The subsidiary structure limits liability contagion. An adverse event in Pamlico Sound Operations is contained within that entity. Manufacturing's molds and IP, Technology's patents, and Holdings' balance sheet are not automatically exposed. This is not a scheme to evade legitimate claims — it is the standard architecture of any well-run infrastructure company. Airlines separate their operating companies from their holding companies. Energy companies separate their pipelines from their production assets. PamliEcoConnect applies the same principle to maritime operations.

Investors benefit from this directly. Their capital, held at the Holdings level, is not the first line of exposure to operational liability. It is protected by the entity structure and by the insurance programs maintained at each operating subsidiary.

Founder Continuity and Governance Stability

PamliEcoConnect's value is inseparable from the expertise, relationships, and vision of its founding team. Jay Phillips's knowledge of Pamlico Sound, Hatteras Island maritime culture, USCG regulatory pathways, and foiling catamaran construction is not transferable to a generic management team. John Elion's 30-year Chesapeake Bay network and his relationships within the five-county consortium are not available on the open market.

The dual-class share structure — Class B shares for founders carrying 10 votes per share, Class A shares for investors carrying 1 vote per share — ensures that the people who built this company and who understand it most deeply retain the authority to execute its vision through the critical early years of growth. This is not a mechanism to ignore investor interests. Standard investor protections — preferred stock liquidation preferences, anti-dilution provisions, and narrowly defined protective vetoes on specific material decisions — remain fully intact.

It is a mechanism to ensure that the company is not redirected by short-term financial pressures in ways that would damage the long-term platform value that makes the investment attractive in the first place. The same dual-class structure is used by Google, Facebook, Snap, Lyft, and the majority of high-growth technology companies that have gone public in the last two decades. It is a recognized and accepted governance tool for founder-led ventures where the founding vision is a core asset.

Investors who take a long-term view — which is the only appropriate view for an infrastructure venture in the maritime clean transportation space — will recognize that founder continuity is a feature, not a risk.

The Jones Act as a Governance Anchor

The Jones Act requirement that vessels be U.S.-built, U.S.-owned, and U.S.-crewed for domestic passenger service creates a governance anchor that no investor action can remove. As long as PamliEcoConnect operates passenger ferry service on U.S. coastal waters, it must maintain Jones Act compliance. That compliance requires U.S. ownership of the vessels and the operating company, U.S. domicile of the manufacturing entity, and U.S. citizenship of crew.

This means the company cannot be sold to a foreign acquirer without triggering Jones Act recertification requirements that would effectively end the ferry operations. The Jones Act compliance requirement is not a burden — it is a natural protection against the most disruptive form of hostile corporate action, foreign acquisition and outsourcing of operations.

A Philosophy in One Sentence

PamliEcoConnect is structured so that the people who built it can keep building it, the communities it serves can count on it, and the investors who backed it can trust that no single adverse event or short-term financial pressure can undo what has been built.

Infrastructure Costs of Good Governance

Transparency requires acknowledging that this governance architecture has real costs. Annual corporate maintenance across all entities, FAR-compliant accounting systems, the mission services partnership agreement, and professional legal and accounting support represent approximately $57,000 in Year 1 rising to approximately $123,000 annually by Year 5 as grant volume and audit requirements scale.

These costs are included in the financial model. They are not hidden. They are the price of building a company that is organized well enough to be trusted with federal grant capital, public infrastructure partnerships, and community obligations. In the context of a Year 5 revenue model exceeding $22 million, they represent less than one percent of revenue. In the context of the mission they protect, they are essential.
